**Unit Title:** Alphabet & Pronunciation
**Grammar Concept:** Italian alphabet, vowel sounds, double consonants (caffè, penna), stress patterns, and common pronunciation rules (e.g. C and G before vowels).
**Vocabulary Concept:** Greetings (Ciao, Buongiorno, Arrivederci), spellings of names, nationalities, and basic numbers 1-20.

**EXHAUSTIVE GRAMMAR GUIDE**

### Italian Alphabet

The Italian alphabet consists of 21 letters: A, B, C, D, E, F, G, H, I, L, M, N, O, P, Q, R, S, T, U, V, Z. The letters J and W are not part of the standard Italian alphabet.

### Vowel Sounds

Italian vowels have distinct sounds:

- A: [a] (open, as in "father")
- E: [e] (closed, as in "pet") or [ɛ] (open, as in "bet")
- I: [i] (closed, as in "hit") or [ɪ] (open, as in "bit")
- O: [o] (closed, as in "go") or [ɔ] (open, as in "law")
- U: [u] (closed, as in "boot")

### Double Consonants

Double consonants are pronounced with a longer duration of the single sound:

- Caffè: /kafˈfɛ/ (CAF-feh)
- Penna: /penˈna/ (PEH-nah)

### Stress Patterns

Italian stress patterns follow these rules:

- The penultimate syllable is stressed in most words.
- If a word ends with -io, the stress falls on the last syllable.

### Common Pronunciation Rules

- C and G before vowels are pronounced as [tʃ] (ch) or [dʒ] (j), respectively:
  - Casa: /ˈkaza/ (KAH-zah)
  - Giorno: /ˈdʒorno/ (JORN-oh)

### Formal vs Informal Usage

Italian has formal and informal pronouns:

- Formal: Lei, Lui, Le
- Informal: Tu, Lui, Te

**Contextual Example Sentences**

1. Io parlo italiano da cinque anni. (I have been speaking Italian for five years.)
2. Lei è molto gentile con me. (She is very kind to me.)
3. Noi andiamo al cinema stasera. (We are going to the movies tonight.)
4. Loro parlano di politica in questo momento. (They are talking about politics right now.)
5. Io non parlo inglese molto bene. (I don't speak English very well.)

**COMMON MISTAKES & SYSTEMATIC ERRORS**

1. **False Friends**: English speakers often confuse "costare" with "to cost", but in Italian, it means "to be expensive".
2. **Pronoun Placement**: English speakers tend to place pronouns at the end of sentences, whereas in Italian, they are usually placed before the verb.
3. **Wrong Auxiliary Verbs**: English speakers often use "avere" (to have) instead of "essere" (to be) as an auxiliary verb for the present perfect tense.

**PRACTICAL DIALOGUE SCENARIO**

**Dialogue:**

1. Ciao, come stai?
2. Sto bene, grazie.
3. Sono felice di conoscerti.
4. Anch'io sono felice di conoscerti.
5. Dove vivi?
6. Vivo a Roma.
7. Ah, bella città!
8. Sì, è una città meravigliosa.

**English Translation:**

1. Hello, how are you?
2. I'm fine, thank you.
3. I'm happy to meet you.
4. Likewise, I'm happy to meet you too.
5. Where do you live?
6. I live in Rome.
7. Ah, what a beautiful city!
8. Yes, it's a wonderful city.
