## Part 1: The Conversational Scenario (The Dialogue)

This dialogue takes place between two friends, Marco and Sofia, who are meeting up to buy food and order coffee.

### 🇮🇹 Italian Dialogue

**Marco:** Ciao Sofia! Come stai oggi?
**Sofia:** Ciao Marco! Sto bene, grazie. E tu?
**Marco:** Anch'io bene. Allora, andiamo al supermercato?
**Sofia:** Sì, andiamo. Cosa vogliamo comprare oggi?
**Marco:** Io voglio del pane e della frutta. Tu vuoi qualcosa di specifico?
**Sofia:** Io voglio del formaggio e delle uova. Non voglio la carne oggi.
**Marco:** Ok, nessun problema. Io voglio delle mele e del latte.
**Sofia:** Perfetto. E per il caffè?
**Marco:** Io voglio un espresso, per favore. E tu?
**Sofia:** Io voglio un cappuccino. Quanto costa il cappuccino?
**Marco:** Il cappuccino costa due euro. E il pane?
**Sofia:** Il pane costa un euro e cinquanta.
**Marco:** Perfetto. Allora, andiamo insieme.
**Sofia:** Sì, andiamo.

---

### 🇬🇧 English Translation

**Marco:** Hi Sofia! How are you today?
**Sofia:** Hi Marco! I am well, thank you. And you?
**Marco:** I am well too. So, shall we go to the supermarket?
**Sofia:** Yes, let's go. What do we want to buy today?
**Marco:** I want some bread and fruit. Do you want something specific?
**Sofia:** I want some cheese and eggs. I do not want meat today.
**Marco:** Okay, no problem. I want some apples and milk.
**Sofia:** Perfect. And for the coffee?
**Marco:** I want an espresso, please. And you?
**Sofia:** I want a cappuccino. How much does the cappuccino cost?
**Marco:** The cappuccino costs two euros. And the bread?
**Sofia:** The bread costs one euro and fifty cents.
**Marco:** Perfect. So, let's go together.
**Sofia:** Yes, let's go.

### ⚙️ Grammar Focus: Negation with *Non*

In this dialogue, we used the structure **"Non voglio"** (I do not want). This is the fundamental way to express negation in Italian.

**Structure:** Subject Pronoun + **Non** + Verb (infinitive form)

*   **Io non voglio** (I do not want)
*   **Tu non vuoi** (You do not want)
*   **Lui/Lei non vuole** (He/She does not want)

**Key Takeaway:** Unlike English, where we use "do not" or "don't," Italian places the negation directly before the verb.

## Part 3: Key Conversational Structures & Cultural Notes

As an A1 learner, mastering these structures will allow you to participate in basic everyday interactions.

### 1. Asking for Price: *Quanto costa...?*

**Structure:** *Quanto costa + [Noun]?* (How much does [Noun] cost?)

*   **Usage:** This is mandatory when shopping or ordering. It is a direct, functional question.
*   **Cultural Note:** Italians are generally direct when discussing prices, but politeness is added by using *per favore* (please) when making the request.

### 2. Expressing Desire/Preference: *Voglio / Non voglio*

**Structure:** *Io voglio/non voglio + [Noun].* (I want/I do not want [Noun].)

*   **Usage:** Used to state personal needs or choices. This is the most basic way to communicate wants.
*   **Cultural Note:** In casual settings, stating a simple desire is perfectly acceptable. For more complex preferences (e.g., "I prefer X to Y"), you will learn the comparative structures later (B1/B2).

### 3. Making Polite Requests: *Per favore*

**Structure:** Adding *per favore* (or *per piacere*) to a request.

*   **Usage:** Essential for politeness. It softens the command or request, making the interaction friendly.
*   **Cultural Note:** Using politeness markers is crucial in Italian social interaction. Always use *per favore* when asking for something, especially in a service setting like a café.

### 4. Simple Agreement/Confirmation: *Perfetto / Sì*

**Structure:** Using simple adverbs to confirm understanding or agreement.

*   **Usage:** Used to confirm that the plan is acceptable or that the information received is correct.
*   **Cultural Note:** Italians often use these simple, positive affirmations to signal smooth communication. *Perfetto* (Perfect) is a very common, versatile word for agreement.
