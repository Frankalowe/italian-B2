### Detailed Pronunciation Rules:

#### 1. Double Consonants (Doppie)
In Italian, double consonants (like *tt, cc, ll, ss, pp, gn*) must be held slightly longer than a single consonant. They represent a sustained sound.

*   **Example:** *Caffè* (KAH-fay) – The 'ff' sound is sustained.
*   **Example:** *Panna* (PAHN-nah) – The 'nn' sound is sustained.

#### 2. The C and G Rule (C e G prima di E/I)
When the letter **C** or **G** is followed by the vowels **E** or **I**, they are pronounced like the hard English **CH** and **J** sounds, respectively.

*   **C before E/I $\rightarrow$ Sounds like CH:**
    *   *Cielo* (Sky) $\rightarrow$ Sounds like **CHIE**-lo
    *   *Città* (City) $\rightarrow$ Sounds like **CHIT**-tah
*   **G before E/I $\rightarrow$ Sounds like J:**
    *   *Giro* (Turn) $\rightarrow$ Sounds like **JEE**-ro
    *   *Gita* (Trip) $\rightarrow$ Sounds like **JEE**-tah

#### 3. Basic Stress Pattern (L'accento)
Italian stress is generally predictable. For A1 learners, focus on the general rule:

*   **Stress usually falls on the penult (second-to-last) syllable.**
    *   *A-mer-i-ca* (America) $\rightarrow$ Stress on **-ri-**
    *   *Ca-ffe* (Coffee) $\rightarrow$ Stress on **CAFF**-e
